01Brief + Origin

A year of inert data
finally made useful.

I've been tracking sleep and stress data for eighteen months. The numbers were always there — they just never told me what to do about them. Every health dashboard I used gave me graphs. None gave me decisions.

The question I kept asking: "OK — so what should I actually do today?" That question became PulsePilot. Not another tracking layer. A single, honest recommendation, with a clear statement of how confident the AI is and exactly what it wants to touch.

The core UX bet: explain before you automate. Every suggestion comes with a reasoning card. Every automated change is reversible. The system graduates from reading → suggesting → acting — only as far as you allow it.

The problem

Health AI fails when users feel surprised by automation. Trust needs transparency, and transparency can't be buried three screens deep.

  • Dashboards optimised for data density create decision fatigue at the worst moment
  • Binary on/off automation controls break the moment the AI gets something wrong
  • Explainability is usually optional — it should be the default

Scope

  • Desktop dashboard — the full decision surface
  • Mobile mirror — same logic, thumb-safe layout
  • Live interactive prototype — full state machine
02Primary Persona
Ryo Watanabe
Startup Founder · Remote · 34 · Tokyo / Berlin
"I know I'm burning out. I just can't afford to stop until the sprint ends."
Relationship with data

Trusts quantified metrics. Has tracked sleep since 2022. But raw biometric numbers make him anxious — he needs context, not just values.

Tolerance for AI

Will accept AI recommendations if they're explained and reversible. Turns off any automation that acts without warning. Once bitten, twice locked.

Decision window

Checks phone at 7am. Back-to-back calls 9–1pm. The 8–9am gap before the calendar locks is the only viable moment to adjust the day.

Frustration

Spends 20 minutes piecing together what his health data means. Gets the charts, never the "so what." Leaves apps after three days of not acting on anything.

Goal

One reliable signal each morning — is today a push day or a protect day? And a clear path to act on it without disrupting his calendar structure.

Fear

An AI that moves meetings without telling him. Or one he can't undo. Losing control is, to him, worse than being tired.

04Design Decisions
1

Why three autonomy modes instead of a simple on/off toggle?

Binary controls collapse under failure. When an AI acts wrong once, a toggle means users turn off the whole feature and trust evaporates all at once. There's no middle ground to retreat to.

The reasoning

Graduated modes give users a landing zone. Copilot is the critical one: it delivers the recommendation and makes the approval path obvious, so the AI stays useful even when the user isn't ready to delegate. Trust builds incrementally. Moving from Copilot to Agent is something users self-select into, never prompted.

2

Why is the reasoning card visible by default, not behind a toggle?

The instinct was to hide it — it felt like clutter. Three founders I tested with dismissed AI recommendations they didn't understand, even when they were correct.

The reasoning

Legibility is the feature. When Ryo sees "sleep debt: 2.1h · stress trending up · meeting lands in your low-recovery window" he acts. When he just sees "we suggest moving your meeting" he ignores it. The reasoning card is the trust mechanism. Hiding it optimises for cleanliness at the cost of adoption.

3

Why a computed recovery score (0–100) rather than raw biometric values?

Prototype v1 showed HRV, resting HR, temperature deviation, and sleep stages simultaneously. Three out of three users said some version of "this makes me feel worse."

The reasoning

Context collapses anxiety. A single recovery score answers the only question Ryo actually has: "Am I OK today?" Raw values invite obsessive comparison. The score invites a decision. The detail is still accessible — one tap expands it — for users who want to audit the model.
